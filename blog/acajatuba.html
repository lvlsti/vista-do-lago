Picture this: you wake up to the sound of the Rio Negro lapping against the dock. No alarm, no traffic, no notifications that matter. Outside your window, the jungle begins where the lawn ends, and somewhere out on the water a pink dolphin breaks the surface. This is Acajatuba — and most people who travel to the Amazon have never heard of it.

Where Exactly Is Acajatuba?

Acajatuba sits on the right bank of the Rio Negro — the world's largest blackwater river — about 75 km from Manaus, inside the Rio Negro Sustainable Development Reserve. Getting there is part of the experience: 1 hour and 30 minutes by speedboat across dark, mirror-like water flanked by unbroken rainforest, or 4 hours by regional river boat if you want to slow down and watch the jungle pass. Either way, by the time you arrive, the city feels very far away.

A Community That Lives on Its Own Terms

The official name is Comunidade Nossa Senhora do Perpétuo Socorro. Ask anyone on the river and they will call it the Comunidade do Corinthians. In the Amazon interior, each riverside community has its team — and here, the passion for Corinthians is woven into daily life. On match days the whole place pulses. Strangers become family over a goal.

Around 200 people call Acajatuba home. There is a Catholic church and several evangelical ones, a municipal school, a health post, a community centre, a craft shop and a football pitch. On any given afternoon you might find a game already going — and if you show up with energy, you will almost certainly be waved onto the field.

What You Will Actually Do Here

Forget the highlight-reel version of the Amazon. Here, the experiences are quieter, realer and stay with you longer:

  • Walk the community. The locals are warm, curious and genuinely welcoming. A morning stroll has a way of turning into a long conversation on someone's porch with a cup of local coffee in your hand.
  • Cross the wooden bridge. One of the community's proudest structures — a tall wooden bridge built to connect neighbours across the water, especially during flood season when the river rises and reclaims the land.
  • Buy from the craft shop. Everything is handmade using Amazonian materials by local artisan families. When you buy here, the money stays here.
  • Join a game. Football or volleyball, it does not matter. If the field is busy, you are already invited.
  • Go deep into the jungle. With a local guide, you enter the forest and start seeing what you would miss alone — medicinal plants, animal tracks, sounds you cannot name yet but will never forget.
  • Swim in the Rio Negro. The dark water is acidic enough to keep piranha and parasites out, which means you can swim freely alongside pink river dolphins — the boto vermelho — in one of the most surreal experiences the Amazon offers.
  • Spot caimans after dark. Night on the river is a different world. A guided boat ride with spotlights reveals caimans resting along the banks, eyes catching the light. It is thrilling in the best possible way.

Come in July for the Festival de Verão

Every July, as the river drops and sandy beaches emerge from the water, Acajatuba celebrates its Festival de Verão — the Summer Festival. Music, regional food, cultural activities and the kind of collective joy that only a tight-knit community can produce. If you can time your visit for July, you will see a side of Amazonian life that no guided tour can manufacture.

The Rio Negro: Why It Feels Different

The Rio Negro's deep colour comes from tannins — the same compounds that darken tea — released by decomposing vegetation along the riverbanks. The result is a river with naturally acidic water: fewer mosquitoes than you expect, no piranha, and a surface so still at golden hour that it turns into a perfect black mirror reflecting the sky above. You have never swum in anything quite like it.
