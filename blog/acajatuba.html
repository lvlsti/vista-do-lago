Acajatuba: The Hidden Amazon Village on the Rio Negro

June 2026 · Vista do Lago Jungle Lodge · 5 min read

There are places in the Amazon that do not appear on tourist maps, do not trend on social media and do not need to. Acajatuba is one of them. Sitting on the right bank of the Rio Negro — the world's largest blackwater river — about 75 km from Manaus, this small riverside community of around 200 people carries within it something rare: a way of life shaped entirely by the jungle, the river and centuries of Amazonian tradition.

Where Exactly Is Acajatuba?

Acajatuba is located within the Rio Negro Sustainable Development Reserve (RDS Rio Negro), a protected conservation area on the margins of the Rio Negro in the state of Amazonas, Brazil. From Manaus, the journey takes approximately 1 hour and 30 minutes by speedboat, or around 4 hours by regional river boat — a slow, beautiful crossing across dark mirror-like waters flanked by dense rainforest.

The Community: Nossa Senhora do Perpétuo Socorro

The official name is Comunidade Nossa Senhora do Perpétuo Socorro, but ask anyone in the region and they will call it the Comunidade do Corinthians. In the Amazon, riverside communities often pledge allegiance to a football club — and here, the passion for Corinthians runs deep. When there is a match, the whole community feels it.

Beyond the football colours, the community has a Catholic church and several evangelical churches, a municipal school, a health post (UBS), a community centre, a craft shop and a football field where visitors are sometimes invited to join a game of football or volleyball with the locals — one of those spontaneous Amazon moments that no tour package can guarantee.

What to Do in Acajatuba

Acajatuba is not the kind of place where you follow a set itinerary. The best experiences happen naturally. Still, here is what awaits you:

  • Walk through the community — The locals are known for their warmth and openness. A stroll through the streets often turns into conversation, laughter and an impromptu invitation.
  • Cross the wooden bridge — One of the community's proudest structures is a tall wooden bridge built to ease daily movement, especially during the flood season when water levels rise dramatically.
  • Visit the craft shop — Local artisans produce handmade pieces using Amazonian materials. Buying directly supports the families who make them.
  • Play football or volleyball — If the locals are out on the field, chances are they will wave you over. Bring the energy.
  • Explore the jungle trails — With a local guide, you venture into the surrounding rainforest to learn about medicinal plants, jungle survival and the sounds of the Amazon at close range.
  • Swim with pink river dolphins — The Rio Negro is home to the boto vermelho, the Amazon pink dolphin. The dark, acidic waters of the Rio Negro are naturally free of piranha and parasites, making it one of the safest rivers in the Amazon to swim in.
  • Night caiman spotting — After dark, the river becomes another world. Guided boat trips with spotlights reveal caimans resting along the banks — a sight that is as thrilling as it sounds.

The Festival de Verão do Acajatuba

Every July, Acajatuba comes alive with the Festival de Verão do Acajatuba — the Summer Festival, named after the dry season when the river recedes and sandy beaches emerge from the water. Music, local food, cultural activities and community pride fill the air. If you can plan your trip to coincide with July, the experience is entirely different from any other time of year.

Why the Rio Negro Is Special

The Rio Negro's water gets its dark colour from tannins released by decomposing vegetation — the same process that makes tea dark. This acidity creates a unique ecosystem: fewer mosquitoes, no piranha, and water so clear and calm that at sunset it reflects the sky like a black mirror. Swimming here feels different from any other river on earth.
